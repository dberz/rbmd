The alarming rise of cancer rates in younger populations is occupying a lot of space in my head these days. Today, I talk about why that is, what we can do to prevent it and how to reverse course. The Wall Street Journal reported last week that in the US, despite an overall decrease in cancer deaths—primarily due to reduced lung cancer rates from lower smoking rates—other cancer types like colorectal, breast, and melanoma are increasingly affecting those under 50 . The American Cancer Society notes a particular increase in colorectal cancer in people born after 1950, with 20% of cases now diagnosed under 50, up from 11% in the 1990s. A report from the British Medical Journal last fall revealed a stark reality: a 79.1% increase in early-onset cancers (diagnosed before age 50) and nearly a 30% rise in related deaths from 1990 to 2019. My journey into medicine began with my grandmother, who died unnecessarily young from colon cancer. This could have been prevented but she lacked good primary care and didn’t get a screening in time.
